PONV — Apfel Score & Triple Prophylaxis
05
Apfel Score (1 point each)
  • Female sex
  • Non-smoker
  • History of PONV or motion sickness
  • Postoperative opioid use
  • Score 0–1 = low; 2 = moderate; 3–4 = high risk
Prophylaxis by Risk
  • Low (0–1)No routine prophylaxis; ondansetron rescue only
  • Moderate (2)1–2 agents: dexamethasone 8 mg + ondansetron 4 mg
  • High (3–4)Triple: dex + ondansetron + droperidol 0.625 mg (or TIVA instead of volatile)
  • Rescue PACUOndansetron 4 mg IV; metoclopramide 10 mg; cyclizine 50 mg
Non-pharmacological
  • TIVA (propofol) vs volatile — ↓ PONV by ~25%
  • Avoid N₂O — ↑ PONV, bowel distension
  • Minimise opioids — multimodal analgesia
  • Adequate IV hydration intraoperatively
  • P6 acupressure (Neiguan point)
  • Scopolamine patch 1.5 mg trans-dermal pre-op

LAST — Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity
02
⚠ Critical Emergency — Intralipid immediately available at all block locations
LAST can present up to 30 min after injection. Subarachnoid injection from eye block → immediate total spinal: intubate, ventilate, support CVS.
Progressive Signs
  • Peri-oral tingling, light-headedness, metallic taste
  • Tremor, tinnitus, blurred vision
  • Twitching → loss of consciousness
  • Seizures → cardiovascular collapse / arrest
  • CVS toxicity: bupivacaine > ropivacaine > lignocaine
Management
  • Stop injectionCall for help; ABC; 100% O₂; secure IV access
  • Intralipid 20%1.5 mL/kg bolus IV then 15 mL/kg/h; max 10 mL/kg first 30 min
  • SeizuresMidazolam 0.1 mg/kg or thiopentone; avoid propofol if CVS unstable
  • Cardiac arrestCPR >1 h may be required; transfer ICU; ECMO if refractory

Malignant Hyperthermia
03
⚠ MH — Life-Threatening Emergency
Triggered by volatile agents and suxamethonium. Incidence ~1:10,000–1:100,000. Autosomal dominant RYR1 mutation most common.
Clinical Features
  • Early↑ EtCO₂ (first sign), masseter spasm after sux, ↑ HR, ↑ temp (>0.5°C/15 min)
  • LateHyperthermia (>40°C), metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalaemia, DIC, cardiac arrest
  • MH-susceptiblePrior MH episode, positive IVCT, RYR1 mutation, King-Denborough syndrome, central core disease
Management
  • Stop triggerRemove volatile; change circuit; increase fresh gas flow; use charcoal filters if available
  • Dantrolene2.5 mg/kg IV bolus → repeat up to 10 mg/kg total; mix each vial with 60 mL sterile water
  • CoolingIV cold saline; ice packs to axillae, groin; cooling blanket; target temp <38.5°C
  • Treat consequencesHyperkalaemia (Ca gluconate, bicarb, insulin/dextrose); acidosis (bicarb); coagulopathy (FFP/cryo)
  • ICUMinimum 24 h; dantrolene 1 mg/kg Q6H × 24–48 h; monitor CK, urine myoglobin, K⁺

Severe PET Criteria (any one of)
BP ≥160/110 on 2 occasions ≥15 min apart; proteinuria >5 g/24 h; pulmonary oedema; oliguria <500 mL/24 h; thrombocytopenia <100 × 10⁹/L; hepatic dysfunction; neurological symptoms; fetal compromise.
Antihypertensive Management
  • Labetalol IV20 mg IV bolus over 2 min; repeat 40 mg → 80 mg Q10 min; max 300 mg; infusion 20–160 mg/h — first-line
  • Hydralazine IV5 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 5–10 mg Q20 min; max 20 mg; onset 10–20 min
  • Nifedipine PO10–20 mg oral (immediate release); repeat after 30 min if needed
  • Target BPSystolic 140–155; diastolic 90–105 — avoid rapid ↓ (uteroplacental insufficiency)
Magnesium Sulphate (Eclampsia / Seizure Prophylaxis)
  • Loading dose4 g IV over 20 min (via infusion pump — not rapid bolus)
  • Maintenance1 g/h IV infusion; continue 24 h post-delivery or last seizure
  • Toxicity signsLoss of DTRs (serum Mg 3.5–5 mmol/L) → respiratory depression (5–6.5) → cardiac arrest (>7.5)
  • MonitorDTRs Q1h; RR Q1h; UO ≥25 mL/h; serum Mg if concerns
  • AntidoteCalcium gluconate 1 g IV over 3 min (if toxicity)
Regional vs GA for PET
  • Spinal preferredIf platelets >75 × 10⁹/L, no coagulopathy, no rapidly evolving signs — safer than GA
  • Epidural preferredGradual BP lowering; avoids pressor response of intubation; top-up for CS if labour epidural in situ
  • GA concernsLaryngeal oedema → difficult airway; pressor response to intubation → ↑ BP; airway oedema post-extubation
  • If GA neededBlunt intubation response: alfentanil 10–20 mcg/kg or remifentanil 1 mcg/kg before laryngoscopy; video laryngoscope; have surgical airway kit ready

Anticipate massive haemorrhage. Ideally deliver in a centre with interventional radiology, cell salvage, and MHP capability. Consultant anaesthetist and haematologist involved from booking.

Labour Epidural — Loading, Maintenance & Troubleshooting
EpiduralLabour
▶
Loading (0.2% Ropivacaine)
  1. Check indication, Hb, platelets, coags; consent; skin LA
  2. Test dose 6 mL before securing catheter
  3. 1 mL post-fixation to check patency
  4. Wait 5 min → check no motor block, check BP → 5 mL
  5. Wait 5 min → 5 mL; total 14–20 mL
  6. Aim 45 min skin-infiltration to comfort; do not start PCEA until 30 min after loading
Troubleshooting
  • Check sequenceMachine → catheter → patient → block level
  • Sudden stopSuspect migration — re-site
  • One-sidedLie affected side down; bolus 5 mL 0.25% bupivacaine
  • Patchy/failingReplace early — a partially working epidural will fail at CS. Do not flog it under time pressure
  • Breakthrough5 mL 0.25% bupivacaine or 5 mL 2% lidocaine + flush
Difficult Obstetric Airway — Management Plan
GAFailed intubation
▶
⚠ Maternal Oxygenation is the Priority — Not Intubation
The obstetric airway is more difficult than the general population — higher Mallampati, oedema, breast enlargement, reduced FRC. Incidence of failed intubation ~1:390 GA CS (10× general surgical).
Pre-op Airway Planning
  • AssessMallampati, mouth opening, neck mobility, thyromental distance; repeat assessment in labour (oedema evolves)
  • EquipmentVL as first-line (particularly high BMI, short neck, Mallampati 3–4); bougie; supraglottic device (i-gel size 4); surgical airway kit opened
  • Pre-oxygenate3 min tidal breathing / ETO₂ >0.85; ramped position (ear to sternal notch); high-flow nasal O₂ throughout
  • Awake FOIConsider if anticipated extremely difficult airway — coordinate with experienced team; nebulised LA; nasal approach preferred in obstetrics
Failed Intubation Algorithm (Obstetric)
  1. Call for help immediately; do not make more than 2 intubation attempts
  2. Maintain oxygenation — face mask; 2-person technique; THRIVE if available
  3. Insert supraglottic airway (i-gel or ProSeal LMA size 4) if mask ventilation difficult
  4. Declare "cannot intubate" — decision: wake up patient OR proceed with SGA if immediately life-threatening (Cat 1)
  5. If proceeding: use i-gel in left lateral tilt; cricoid pressure modified; low aspiration risk = no cricoid
  6. Cannot intubate, cannot oxygenate (CICO): emergency front-of-neck access — scalpel cricothyroidotomy; bougie-guided

ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy)
Remote locationUnprotected airway
▶
Key Considerations & Contraindications
  • AirwayUnprotected; remote location; check all equipment independently
  • PhysiologySympathetic surge at stimulus → ↑ HR, ↑ BP; parasympathetic at onset → bradycardia
  • ↑ IOP/ICPRelevant comorbidities — ↑ IOP, ↑ IGP, ↑ ICP during seizure
  • Absolute CIMI <3 months; CVA <1 month; phaeochromocytoma
  • Relative CI↑ ICP; severe cardiac disease; aortic/cerebral aneurysm; sux apnoea / MH risk
Technique (Serial ECT)
  1. Fast list; confirm no new medical issues; note previous propofol/sux dose
  2. IV access; NIBP, SpO₂, ECG; face-mask pre-oxygenation
  3. Propofol = previous dose − 20–30 mg; wait; top-up to loss of verbal contact (LOV)
  4. Suxamethonium same dose; flush IV
  5. Support airway; hyperventilate (↓ seizure threshold → better quality)
  6. ECT stimulus when fasciculations stop; support airway ± OPA post-stim
  7. Hudson mask + leave room if stable and spontaneously ventilating
Rescue
  • HypertensionEsmolol 20–30 mg IV at stimulus
  • BradycardiaAtropine 0.6 mg IV
  • Prolonged seizure>120 s → midazolam 2–4 mg IV; secure airway
  • Failed seizure<15 s → discuss with psychiatrist
NMB Alternative (No Sux)
  • MH / sux-apnoeaMivacurium 0.15 mg/kg IV (short-acting NDMR) or rocuronium 0.6 mg/kg + sugammadex reversal at end
  • MonitoringTOF monitoring even for ECT — confirm fasciculations/block
Drug Interactions
  • Lithium → prolonged sux/NMB block; reduce NMB dose
  • MAOIs → avoid pethidine; caution with sympathomimetics
  • Clozapine → seizure threshold ↓; longer seizures possible
  • Benzodiazepines → ↑ seizure threshold; withhold morning dose

Valve Surgery (AVR / MVR / Repair)
CPBValvular
▶
Aortic Stenosis (AS) — Principles
  • PathophysiologyFixed outflow obstruction; LVH; dependent on heart rate (avoid tachycardia), sinus rhythm, adequate preload
  • InductionSlow, careful titration; avoid SVR drops (propofol large bolus CI); etomidate preferred; maintain HR 60–80
  • AvoidRapid vasodilation; tachycardia; AF; hypovolaemia
  • Post-CPBSudden ↓ afterload after valve replacement — LV now working into normal SVR; may need inotrope support initially
Aortic Regurgitation (AR) — Principles
  • PathophysiologyVolume overload; dilated LV; forward flow dependent on HR (slightly faster = ↓ regurgitant time)
  • Haemodynamic goalsHR 80–100 (avoid bradycardia → ↑ regurgitant volume); avoid ↑ SVR; maintain preload
  • InductionBalanced; avoid bradycardia-inducing agents
Mitral Stenosis (MS) — Principles
  • PathophysiologyFixed LV filling; LA dilation; AF common; pulmonary hypertension
  • GoalsSlow HR (↑ diastolic filling time); sinus rhythm preferred; avoid ↑ pulmonary pressure (hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis, N₂O)
  • AvoidTachycardia; hypoxia; N₂O; excessive fluid
Mitral Regurgitation (MR) — Principles
  • GoalsMaintain forward flow: slightly ↑ HR; ↓ SVR (vasodilators); avoid ↑ SVR or bradycardia
  • Post-repairRepair preferred over replacement; TOE confirms leaflet coaptation after discontinuing bypass; MR grade assessed
  • RV function↑ pulmonary pressures pre-op → RV vulnerable post-CPB; milrinone/vasopressin combination if RV failure

One-Lung Ventilation (OLV) — Key Challenge
Hypoxaemia during OLV is common (SpO₂ may drop to 85–90%). Optimise shunt physiology: HPV, position, CPAP to non-dependent lung. Communicate with surgeon before CPAP manoeuvres.
Lung Isolation
  • DLT (preferred)Left-sided DLT in most cases (37F female; 39F male); confirm with FOB after intubation and after positioning; recheck after repositioning
  • Right-sided DLTLeft-sided lung disease, left pneumonectomy, left thoracic aortic surgery, left mainstem lesion
  • Bronchial blockerAlternative to DLT: Arndt/Cohen blocker; useful when DLT not feasible (difficult airway, tracheostomy); slower deflation
  • ConfirmationFlexible bronchoscopy mandatory to confirm position — visual check of cuffs; correct lumen ventilating correct lung
OLV Management
  • TV during OLV4–5 mL/kg IBW (lower than two-lung); PEEP 5–10 cmH₂O; RR ↑ to maintain normocapnia
  • FiO₂Start at 1.0 during OLV; wean to 0.5 if SpO₂ ≥95%
  • Hypoxaemia algorithm(1) Check DLT position with FOB; (2) CPAP 5 cmH₂O to non-dependent lung; (3) ↑ FiO₂ to 1.0; (4) Intermittent reinflation; (5) TPVL (HPV); (6) surgical ligation of PA
  • HPVHypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction — beneficial reflex; volatile agents blunt HPV; TIVA (propofol) preserves HPV; use propofol/remifentanil TCI for thoracic procedures

Awake Fibre-Optic Intubation (AFOI)
Anticipated difficultGold standard
▶
Indications
  • Predicted very difficult or impossible intubation under GA
  • Unstable cervical spine (trauma, RA, Down's)
  • Significant airway pathology: tumour, abscess, oedema, burn
  • Morbid obesity with multiple predictors
  • Previous failed intubation with documented difficult airway
  • Severe OSA with Mallampati 4 + small mouth + restricted neck
Equipment
  • ScopeFlexible bronchoscope (adult 4–6 mm OD); well-lubricated, tested
  • ETTLoaded on scope; size 7.0–8.0 (prewarmed in warm saline → softer)
  • SuctionYankauer; working scope suction channel
  • Nasal routePreferred for ease; xylometazoline 0.1% nasal spray + co-phenylcaine (LA + vasoconstriction)
  • MonitoringSpO₂, ECG, NIBP; supplemental O₂ throughout via nasal prong or high-flow nasal
Airway Preparation & LA
  1. Consent and explain procedure clearly — patient cooperation is essential
  2. Antisialagogue: glycopyrrolate 0.2–0.4 mg IM 30 min before (dry secretions)
  3. Nasal: xylometazoline spray → co-phenylcaine spray → nasopharyngeal airway with lidocaine gel 2%
  4. Oropharynx: lidocaine spray 10% to soft palate, uvula, tongue base — 4 actuations (max 4 mg/kg total LA)
  5. Subglottic LA: transtracheal injection 2 mL 2% lidocaine through cricothyroid membrane (cough = good) OR spray-as-you-go (SAYG) through scope
  6. Sedation (if used): remifentanil TCI 1–2 ng/mL Ce OR dexmedetomidine 0.5–1 mcg/kg over 10 min then 0.2–0.7 mcg/kg/h — maintain cooperation
Technique
  1. Scope in right hand; ETT loaded; nasal route preferred; advance under vision
  2. Identify turbinates → nasopharynx → uvula → epiglottis
  3. Pass under epiglottis (lifting with scope tip); advance through cords
  4. Visualise carina — confirms tracheal placement
  5. Railroad ETT over scope with gentle rotation (bevel facing posteriorly); remove scope
  6. Confirm EtCO₂ waveform; secure ETT; induce anaesthesia
SAYG — Spray As You Go
  • 2% lidocaine injected through scope suction channel as scope advances
  • 1–2 mL at each anatomical landmark: base of tongue, supraglottic, cords, subglottic
  • Total dose: keep track — max 4 mg/kg; usually 200–400 mg total
  • Allow 30–60 s between applications for effect
Pitfalls
  • Excessive sedation → uncooperative, desaturating patient
  • Secretions obscuring view → scope suction + SAYG lidocaine
  • Vasovagal → bradycardia from tracheal stimulation; atropine available
  • Subglottic oedema → smaller ETT needed; have sizes 6.0 and 6.5 available
  • Failure → have Plan B ready: VL asleep with sedation; surgical airway
After AFOI
  • Confirm patient still cooperative before inducing GA
  • Induce gently (propofol low-dose; avoid sudden apnoea in compromised airway)
  • Document technique in anaesthetic record and patient notes
  • Communicate to PACU and ward: difficult airway patient
Can't Intubate / Can't Oxygenate (CICO) — Emergency FONA
Life-threateningFONA
▶
⚠ CICO — Declare Immediately. Do Not Delay FONA. Every Second Counts.
Failure to act within 5 min = hypoxic brain damage. Do not make a fourth intubation attempt. Call for surgical help while proceeding with scalpel technique.
CICO Recognition
  • Cannot intubate2–3 attempts failed; VL + direct; grade 3–4 view; cannot railroad bougie
  • Cannot oxygenateSpO₂ falling despite mask ± OPA ± NPA ± 2-person technique; SGA insertion failed or inadequate
  • Declare CICO"This is a CICO situation — I am performing front-of-neck access"
Scalpel-Bougie-Tube Technique (DAS recommended)
  1. Extend neck; identify CTM (finger pinch 4-finger technique)
  2. Horizontal stab incision through skin + CTM with 10-blade scalpel (inferior to superior)
  3. Hook finger through incision caudally to maintain airway access
  4. Insert bougie through incision into trachea (caudally); feel tracheal rings
  5. Railroad 6.0 cuffed ETT over bougie; inflate cuff; confirm EtCO₂
  6. Secure ETT; ventilate 100% O₂; call ENT for formal tracheostomy
Needle Cricothyroidotomy (Bridge — Not Definitive)
  • IndicationUnable to immediately perform scalpel technique; paediatric CICO (<8 yr); bridge to surgical airway
  • Technique14G cannula + syringe through CTM; aspirate air to confirm; attach Luer-lock connector; jet ventilation at 1 L/yr age
  • LimitationCannot provide adequate ventilation for >30–45 min; CO₂ accumulates; does not secure airway; proceed to definitive airway
Post-CICO Care
  • Formal tracheostomyENT/surgeon to perform formal tracheostomy to replace emergency cricothyrotomy
  • DebriefingTeam debrief mandatory; complete incident report; inform patient when awake
  • DocumentationDetailed record of events and timeline; MedAlert bracelet / ICE card for patient — "DIFFICULT AIRWAY"

Percutaneous Dilatational Tracheostomy (PDT)
  • IndicationsProlonged ventilation; failed extubation; airway protection; upper airway obstruction (planned)
  • Pre-op checksCoags (INR <1.5; platelets >50); neck anatomy (palpable rings, midline trachea); bronchoscopy confirms position
  • TechniqueBronchoscope through ETT (pull back ETT under vision); Seldinger technique: needle → wire → serial dilation (Ciaglia) → tracheostomy tube
  • Bronchoscopic guidanceMandatory — confirms needle placement between rings 1–3; avoids posterior wall puncture; confirms tube position
  • ComplicationsBleeding; paratracheal placement; posterior wall injury; loss of airway during procedure; surgical emphysema
Surgical Tracheostomy
  • IndicationsEmergency (CICO); complex neck anatomy; coagulopathy; previous neck surgery; paediatric; short neck; goitre
  • PositionShoulder roll; extended neck; maximise CTM / tracheal access
  • TechniqueVertical midline incision; strap muscles divided; thyroid isthmus divided or retracted; incision through rings 2–3; insert Bjork flap or simple opening; insert tube
  • Tube changeFirst change at 5–7 days (once tract formed); have introducers and same + smaller size tubes at bedside; FOB available
  • DecannulationCuff down trials; speaking valve (Passy-Muir); downsize tube; cap trial; decannulate and observe 24 h
Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) — Optimised
Full stomachAspiration risk
▶
RSI Technique
  1. Pre-oxygenation: 3 min tidal breathing OR 8 vital capacity breaths; ETO₂ >0.85; high-flow nasal O₂ throughout (apnoeic oxygenation)
  2. Position: ramped / head-up 20–30° — ↑ FRC, ↓ aspiration if regurgitation occurs
  3. Pre-oxygenation: avoid assisted mask ventilation (distends stomach)
  4. Propofol (or ketamine/etomidate if haemodynamic compromise) + suxamethonium 1.5 mg/kg OR rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg simultaneously
  5. Cricoid pressure: applied at loss of consciousness; 10 N awake → 30 N asleep (Sellick); release if impeding intubation
  6. Intubate at maximal paralysis (fasciculations cease with sux; or 60 s with roc); confirm EtCO₂; release cricoid

TURP — Transurethral Resection of Prostate
SpinalTURP syndrome
▶
TURP Syndrome
Absorption of hypotonic glycine/sorbitol irrigant → hyponatraemia, fluid overload, haemolysis, encephalopathy. Risk ↑ with resection time >60 min or irrigant pressure >40 cmH₂O. Spinal allows early detection (confusion = TURP syndrome).
Technique
  • Spinal preferredAllows early detection of TURP syndrome (patient becomes confused); T10 level adequate; prilocaine 2% 2.5 mL or bupivacaine 0.5% heavy 2.5 mL
  • GA optionLMA; TURP syndrome harder to detect; BIS monitoring; send Na⁺ Q30 min if prolonged
  • PositionLithotomy — careful positioning; compartment syndrome risk with prolonged lithotomy; release legs simultaneously
  • MonitoringUrinary output; serum Na⁺ intraoperatively if resection >45 min; volume of irrigant absorbed estimated from bag difference
TURP Syndrome — Management
  • Na⁺ 120–125Fluid restriction; furosemide 20–40 mg IV; stop surgery ASAP; monitor closely
  • Na⁺ <120 or symptomsHypertonic saline 3% — correct Na⁺ at <1.5 mmol/L/h; avoid rapid correction (osmotic demyelination)
  • SeizuresIV benzodiazepine; airway management; urgent electrolyte correction
  • Bipolar TURP / HOLEPNormal saline irrigant — eliminates TURP syndrome; increasingly standard

Robotic Prostatectomy (RARP)
UrologySteep Trendelenburg
▶
Technique
  • PositionSteep Trendelenburg (30–40°) + pneumoperitoneum — most physiologically challenging part; ICP ↑, IOP ↑, ↓ FRC, ↑ Paw, facial oedema
  • AirwayCOETT; secure extremely well — impossible to reposition with robot docked; check position after Trendelenburg
  • VentilationLung-protective: TV 6 mL/kg IBW; PEEP 5–10; RR ↑; allow permissive hypercapnia (PaCO₂ 5–6 kPa) — avoid very high peak pressures
  • HeadNo direct pressure; gel ring; arms padded and tucked; shoulder supports only if robot requires (brachial plexus risk)
  • OcularTape eyes closed; gel pads; no direct pressure; check Q30–60 min if accessible

  • IOP ↑Avoid in glaucoma / recent eye surgery; known glaucoma → limit steep Trendelenburg time
  • AnalgesiaMultimodal — paracetamol + NSAID (once Hb confirmed stable); port-site LA; surgeon periperitoneal LA; PCA if high pain; lignocaine infusion intraop
  • Post-opDay-case or 1-night stay; IDC usually overnight; early mobilisation; discharge home day 1 in many ERAS pathways

Indications for Post-op ICU
  • Emergency laparotomy / damage control surgery
  • Major vascular surgery (AAA, cardiac surgery)
  • Massive intraoperative haemorrhage (>4 units pRBC)

PainProcedures

Sub-anaesthetic Ketamine Infusion
  • IndicationsChronic neuropathic pain; opioid-refractory pain; post-amputation pain; CRPS; fibromyalgia; central sensitisation
  • Preparation500 mg ketamine in 500 mL NaCl = 1 mg/mL; or 500 mg in 50 mL pump = 10 mg/mL
  • ProtocolInpatient: 0.1–0.5 mg/kg/h × 3–5 days; outpatient/day-case: 0.3–0.5 mg/kg/h × 4–6 h
  • MonitoringSpO₂, BP, HR continuous; verbal contact Q30 min; sedation scale (RASS target 0); dissociation scoring
  • Side effectsDysphoria, hallucinations, nausea — midazolam 1 mg IV PRN; ondansetron 4 mg; diazepam 2 mg PO at start
  • ContraindicationsPoorly controlled psychosis; recent MI (relative); uncontrolled HTN; pregnancy
Epidural Steroid Injection (ESI)
  • IndicationsRadicular pain from disc prolapse; spinal stenosis; failed conservative management; bridge to surgery or rehabilitation
  • ApproachInterlaminar (most common) or transforaminal (targeted); fluoroscopy or CT guidance
  • DrugsDexamethasone 8–12 mg PF (preferred — non-particulate, safer for transforaminal); methylprednisolone 40–80 mg; + bupivacaine 0.25% 3–5 mL
  • TechniqueLoss of resistance saline; confirm epidural space; contrast under fluoroscopy if transforaminal; inject slowly; observe 30–60 min post
  • RisksDural puncture; PDPH; infection; epidural haematoma (stop anticoagulants per ASRA guidelines); steroid side effects; intravascular injection (transforaminal)
  • AnticoagulationASRA guidelines: clopidogrel 7 days; warfarin INR <1.5; LMWH prophylaxis dose 12 h; therapeutic 24 h; DOACs per ASRA guidance
